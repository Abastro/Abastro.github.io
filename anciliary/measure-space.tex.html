Measure Spaces

Posted on 2024-07-10 by Abs

Clearly, there is a need to integrate a function, or measure a domain (a set). Measure theory gives a mathematical framework for such operations.

\(\sigma\)-algebra

It turns out, perhaps unsurprisingly, that not every space can be measured, and not every function can be integrated. \(\sigma\)-algebra of measure spaces encapsulates this concept.

Definition 1. \(\mathcal{A} \in \mathcal{P}(X)\) is

  • an algebra on \(X\) if it is closed under finite union and complement.

  • an \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(X\) if it is closed under countable union and complement.

Remark 1. An algebra is a \(\sigma\)-algebra if it is closed under disjoint countable union.

Consider a category of spaces given by

  1. Objects: \((X, \mathcal{E})\) for \(\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\).

  2. Morphisms: \((X, \mathcal{E}) \xrightarrow{f} (Y, \mathcal{F})\) s.t. \(A \in \mathcal{F}\) implies \(f^{-1} A \in \mathcal{E}\).

Definition 2. \((X, \mathcal{M})\) is called a measure space if \(\mathcal{M}\) is \(\sigma\)-algebra, and morphisms between such spaces are called measurable functions. Sets in \(\mathcal{M}\) is said to be measurable.

Definition 3. \(\mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{E})\) is the terminal \(\sigma\)-alg with a map \((X, \mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{E})) \to (X, \mathcal{E})\). In other words, it is the smallest \(\sigma\)-alg containing \(\mathcal{E}\).

Definition 4. A Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is \(\mathcal{B}_X := \mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{O})\) for collections of open sets \(\mathcal{O}\).

If not mentioned otherwise, a topological space will be given a measure space structure by taking Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra.

Proposition 1. Category of measure spaces have arbitrary product, i.e., \(\prod_{\alpha \in J} X_\alpha\) has a natural \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\bigotimes_{\alpha \in J} \mathcal{M}_\alpha\) with measurable projections \(\pi_\alpha : \prod X_\bullet \to X_\alpha\) for \((X_\alpha, \mathcal{M}_\alpha)_{\alpha \in J}\).

Proof. Take \(\bigotimes_{\alpha \in J} \mathcal{M}_\alpha := M(\bigcup_{\alpha \in J} \{\pi_{\alpha}^{-1} A \mid A \in \mathcal{M}_{\alpha}\})\), which is the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra with projections \(\pi_\alpha\). Then, the cone \(\{ (Y, \mathcal{N}) \to (X_\alpha, \mathcal{M}_\alpha) \}_\alpha\) factors through this space. ◻

Remark 2. If index set is countable, then \(\bigotimes \mathcal{M}_\bullet = \{ \prod E_\bullet | E_\alpha \in \mathcal{E}_\alpha \}\).

Remark 3. For metric spaces given Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra, the projections \(\prod_I X_\bullet \to X_i\) are measurable for finite \(I\), but \(\prod_I X_\bullet\) is only the product if each \(X_i\) has a countable dense subset.

Counts and Measures

Counts

Definition 5. The set of count functions or counts, \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), is the set of bounded measurable functions \(X \to \mathbb{N}\), where \(X\) is given an algebra \(\mathcal{A}\). We may omit \(X\) or \(\mathcal{A}\) if either is obvious from context.

Example 1. An indicator function \(1_A\) of a measurable set \(A\) is a count function \[1_A(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & x \in A \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

Remark 4. Alternatively, we may regard a count as a formal sum of measurable sets. That is, \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X) = \bigoplus_{E \in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{Z}\langle [E] \rangle\), where each \([E]\) correlates to the index function \(1_E\) below.

Proposition 2. \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\) is closed under (finite) addition. Further, if \(\mathcal{A}\) is a \(\sigma\)-alg, it is closed under countable addition whenever bounded.

Proof. Consider \[\left\{ \sum_{i \in I} f_i \leq M \right\} = \bigcup_{M = \sum_{i \in I} m_i} \bigcap_{i \in I} \{ f_i \leq m_i \}.\] If \(I\) is finite, the union and intersection are finite, while if \(I\) is countable, both are countable. It follows that the finite sum is measurable for algebra, and countable sum is measurable for \(\sigma\)-algebra. ◻

Remark 5. Indicator functions serve as a \(\mathbb{N}\)-basis of \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}(X)\).

We give a partial order on \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}(X)\), where \(f \leq g\) if \(f(x) \leq g(x)\) on all \(x \in X\). Then, \(g-f \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}(X)\) if and only if \(f \leq g\).

Proposition 3. Suppose \(\mathcal{F}\) is a set of bounded functions \(f: X \to \mathbb{N}\), closed under addition, and \(f - g \in \mathcal{F}\) for \(f \geq g \in \mathcal{F}\). If \(f \in \mathcal{F}\) implies \(1_{f = 0} \in \mathcal{F}\), such \(\mathcal{F}\) uniquely determines algebra \(\mathcal{A}\) such that \(\mathcal{F} = \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\). Further, if \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under bounded countable addition, then \(\mathcal{A}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.

Proof. Consider \(\mathcal{A} := \{ \{f > 0\} \mid f \in \mathcal{F} \}\), which is an algebra:

  • (Closed under union) \(\{f>0\} \cup \{g>0\} = \{f+g>0\}\),

  • (Closed under complement) \(\{ 1_{f=0} > 0 \} = \{f > 0\}^c\).

Note that if \(E \in \mathcal{A}\), then \(E = \{f > 0\}^c = \{f = 0\}\) for some \(f \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(1_E \in \mathcal{F}\).

It suffices to show that \(\mathcal{F} = \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), since \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}\) determines \(\mathcal{A}\) through indicator functions.

  1. Suppose \(g \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), then \(\{g \geq i\} \in \mathcal{A}\) gives \(1_{g \geq i} \in \mathcal{F}\). Therefore, \(g = \sum_{i=1}^\infty 1_{g \geq i} \in \mathcal{F}\).

  2. Suppose \(g \in \mathcal{F}\). \(g \geq 1_{g > 0} \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(g - 1_{g>0} \in \mathcal{F}\). By induction on bounds, \(\{ g \geq i+1 \} = \{ (g - 1_{g>0}) \geq i \} \in \mathcal{A}\) for each \(i \in \mathbb{N}\). As \(\{g \geq 0\} = X\), this shows that \(\{g \geq j\}\) is measurable, so \(g\) is \(\mathcal{A}\)-measurable.

Lastly, suppose \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under bounded countable addition as well. Then for disjoint sets \(A_i \in \mathcal{A}\), \(1_{\bigsqcup_i^\infty A_i} = \sum_i^\infty 1_{A_i} \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(\bigsqcup_i^\infty A_i \in \mathcal{A}\). Since \(\mathcal{A}\) is established as an algebra, this shows that it is a \(\sigma\)-algebra. ◻

Measures

Measures let us assign “area" onto the space consistently, so that we can “sum up" the count functions.

Definition 6. Given a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M})\), a measure is a map \[\mu : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{M}}(X) \to [0, \infty]\] which is countably additive, i.e., \(\mu(\sum_I f_\bullet) = \sum_I \mu(f_\bullet)\) for at most countable \(I\) whenever defined. Traditional measure can be recovered as \(\mu(A) := \mu(1_A)\).

Note that \(\mu(f) = \sum_k k \cdot \mu(f^{-1}(k))\), so it is easy to see the equivalence of the definitions.

A measure \(\mu\) is trivially monotone, i.e., \(\mu(f) \leq \mu(g)\) if \(f \leq g\).

Proposition 4. A measure \(\mu\) also has properties

  1. (Continuity from above) If \(f_j \uparrow f_\infty\), \(\lim_{j \to \infty} \mu(f_j) = \mu(f_\infty)\).

  2. (Continuity from below) If \(f_j \downarrow f_\infty\) and \(\mu(f_1) < \infty\), \(\lim_{j \to \infty} \mu(f_j) = \mu(f_\infty)\).

Proof. First one follows from countable addition of differences. The second is simply a modification of the first using \(g_j = f_1 - f_j\), where \(\mu(f_1) < \infty\) is required for the limit. ◻

Couple of basic terms to describe measures and its sets.

Definition 7 (Finiteness of a measure). A measure \(\mu\) is

  • finite if \(\mu < \infty\).

  • \(\sigma\)-finite if a countable cover \((E_i)\) has \(\mu|_{E_i} < \infty\).

  • semi-finite if every msb set has a subset of measure \((0, \infty)\).

Definition 8. \(E\) is (\(\mu\)-)null set if \(\mu(E) = 0\).

Definition 9. \(P(x)\) holds (\(\mu\)-)almost everywhere if \(\mu\{ x \mid \neg P(x) \} = 0\).

Remark 6. This gives an alternative notion of equality on \(\mathop{\mathrm{Hom}}(X, Y)\): That is, \(f\) and \(g\) is "roughly equal" if \(f = g\) almost everywhere.

Constructions of a measure

Usually a measure is constructed as an extension. Given a map \(\mu : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X) \to [0, \infty]\) with properties, an extension of it on \(\mathcal{M}\) is a map (measure) \(\bar{\mu} : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{M}}(X) \to [0, \infty]\) such that \(\bar{\mu}|_{\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)} = \mu\).

Completion

Definition 10. \(\mu\) is complete if any subset of \(\mu\)-null set is measurable.

Proposition 5. Consider a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)\). Then, \[\bar{\mathcal{M}} := \left\{ F \in \mathcal{P}(X) \mid E \subset F \subset E' \text{ for } E, E' \in \mathcal{M}, \mu(E' \setminus E) = 0 \right\}\] is a \(\sigma\)-algebra, and \(\mu\) has a unique extension \(\bar{\mu}\) on \(\bar{\mathcal{M}}\) such that \(\bar{\mu}\) is complete.

Proof. Both countable and finite union preserves the condition of \(\bar{\mathcal{M}}\), since countable union of \(\mu\)-null set is \(\mu\)-null. Complement also preserves \(\mathcal{M}\), since \(E^c \setminus E'^c = E' \setminus E\). Hence, \(\bar{\mathcal{M}}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.

Now, being a complete extension mandates \(\bar{\mu}(F) = \mu(E)\) for \(E \subset F \subset E'\) in the condition of \(\mathcal{M}\). This gives well-defined measure on \(\mathcal{M}\), since \(\mu(E)\) is determined by \(F\). ◻

Definition 11. The measure \(\bar{\mu}\) is called the completion of \(\mu\).

Premeasure extension

Often, measures are difficult to construct directly, so it is defined through a weaker construct called premeasure.

Definition 12. For an algebra \(\mathcal{A}\), a premeasure on it is \[\mu_0 : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X) \to \mathbb{R}\] that satisfies precountable additivity: if \(\sum_I f_\bullet \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), \[\mu_0 \left( \sum_I f_\bullet \right) = \sum_I \mu_0(f_\bullet).\]

Definition 13. Consider set of bounded measurable maps \(\tilde{\mathop{\mathrm{Mor}}}(X, \mathbb{N})\). \(\mu^* : \tilde{\mathop{\mathrm{Mor}}}(X, \mathbb{N}) \to [0, \infty]\) is an outer measure if it is monotone and countably subadditive.

Theorem 6. For \(\mathcal{M} = \mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{A})\) and a \(\sigma\)-finite premeasure \(\mu_0\) on \(\mathcal{A}\), one has the unique extension measure \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{M}\).

Proof. We may assume \(\mu_0\) is finite. Indeed, in the \(\sigma\)-finite case, we have \(\{ X_j \}_{j \in J}\) where \(\mu_0 (1_{X_j}) < \infty\), with which we may extend \(\mu_0|_{X_j}\) individually and sum back up onto \(X\).

Step 1: Outer measure

One has an associated outer measure by approximation from above: \[\mu^*(f) := \inf \left\{ \sum_I \mu_0(g_\bullet) \,\middle|\, f \leq \sum_I g_\bullet,\ g_i \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X) \right\}\] On \(\mathcal{A}\), \(\mu^* \leq \mu_0\) is obvious. For \(f \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), consider \(\sum_I g_\bullet \geq f\). Then, taking infimum on \(\sum_I \mu_0(g_\bullet) \geq \mu_0(f)\) gives \(\mu^* \geq \mu_0\), showing \(\mu^* = \mu_0\) on \(\mathcal{A}\). Notice this logic also works for general extension measure \(\nu\) on \(\mathcal{M}\) to prove \(\nu \leq \mu^*\), which will be of use later.

Step 2: \(\mu^*\)-Measurable sets

Consider \(d(f, g) := \mu^* |f - g|\), which is a pseudometric, i.e., it satisfies the triangle inequality. This is well-defined by finiteness, and gives a topological structure on \(\mathop{\mathrm{Mor}}(X, \mathbb{N})\). Notice that if \(f_n \uparrow f\) pointwise, \(f_n \to f\) in this topology as well, as \(\mu^* f_n \uparrow \mu^* f\) gives \(\mu^* (f - f_n) \downarrow 0\).

Claim: The sequential closure \(\mathcal{F} = \overline{\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)}\) is \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{\bar{A}}}(X)\) for some \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{\bar{A}}\).

By Proposition \(\ref{count-recover-algebra}\), it is enough to check the following.

  • (Closed under addition/subtraction) Given by elementwise addition/subtraction of the sequences.

  • (Closed under bounded countable sum) \(\sum_n f_n = f\) implies that partial sum converges to \(f\). Since each partial sum is in \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed, \(f \in \mathcal{F}\).

  • (Contains index functions) For \(f \in \mathcal{F}\), \(1_{f=0} \in \mathcal{F}\) since \(g \mapsto 1_{g=0}\) is Lipschitz.

Step 3: The induced measure and its uniqueness

Notice that any extension measure \(\nu\) on \(\mathcal{\bar{A}}\) is Lipschitz-continuous under the pseudometric: \[|\nu f - \nu g| \leq \nu |f - g| \leq \mu^* |f - g|.\ (\because \nu \leq \mu^*)\] Also, \(\mu^*\) itself is Lipschitz-continuous. As any function in \(\mathcal{F} = \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{\bar{A}}}(X)\) is a limit of counts \(f_n\) on \(\mathcal{A}\), and \(\nu\) agrees with \(\mu^*\) on \(\mathcal{A}\), this shows that \(\nu = \mu^*|_{\mathcal{F}}\), i.e., the uniqueness.

It remains to show that the restriction \(\mu := \mu^*|_{\mathcal{F}}\) is indeed a measure. Note that additivity extends via continuity from \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), and continuity from below is also shown as for \(f_n \uparrow f\), \(\mu f_n \to \mu f\) by continuity. Therefore, \(\mu\) is indeed a measure on \(\mathcal{\bar{A}} \supset \mathcal{M}\).

 ◻

Remark 7. Notice in the above proof, \(\mu\) is complete with respect to \(\mathcal{\bar{A}}\). Indeed, if \(F \subset E\) with \(\mu(1_E) = 0\), one has \(\mu^*(1_F) = 0\), so \(1_F\) is contained in the closure, near \(0 \in \mathcal{F}\). Hence, Theorem \(\ref{premeasure-induced-measure}\) also defines the complete extension measure.

Lebesgue Measure

Now, we give usual measure for \(\mathbb{R}\). We will construct a measure on \((-\infty, \infty]\), and restrict it to \(\mathbb{R}\).

Definition 14. \(\emptyset\), and \((a, b]\) for \(a < b\) are called h-intervals.

Such intervals form an elementary family, i.e., it contains the empty set, it is closed under intersection, and each complement is a finite union of such intervals. This defines an algebra \(\mathcal{A} := \{ \bigsqcup_{i \leq n} I_i \mid I_i \text{ is an h-interval}\}\), and \(\mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{B}_{(-\infty, \infty]}\).

Fix an increasing function \(F : (-\infty, \infty] \to (-\infty, \infty]\) which is right-continuous.

Proposition 7. There is a unique premeasure \(\mu_0 : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(-\infty, \infty] \to [0, \infty]\) satisfying \(\mu_0(1_I) = F(\sup I) - F(\inf I)\) for each h-interval \(I\).

Proof. \(1_I\)’s span \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(\mathbb{R})\), so it is enough to show the well-definedness, for which we only need to track the relations of generators \(\{ 1_I \}\). Such relations are generated by \[\sum_{j \in J} 1_{I_j} = 1_{I_0}\] for countable \(J\), so enough to show that \(\mu_0\) respects this relation.

Consider upper boundaries \(S = \{ \sup I_j \mid j \in J \}\). Notice each element of \(S\) has its associated interval.

Claim: \(S\) with reversed order is well-ordered, so there is a order-reversing bijection \(s : \omega \to S\) from an ordinal \(\omega\).

For \(T \subset S\), \(\sup T \leq \sup S = \sup I_0\), so \(\sup T \in I_0\). Consider the unique interval \(I_{j_0 \in J}\) containing \(\sup T\). One has \(\epsilon > 0\) such that \((\sup T - \epsilon, \sup T] \in I_{j_0}\), so there is \(t \in I_{j_0} \cap T\). Since \(I_{j_0}\) should be an associated interval of \(t\), \(\sup T = t = \max T\) - this proves the claim.

Denote \(s_\gamma = s(\gamma)\) for \(\gamma \in \omega\), and let \(s_\omega = \inf I_0\). Note that for \(\gamma \in \omega\), the associated interval of \(s_\gamma\) is \((s_{\gamma+1}, s_\gamma]\). Indeed, \(s_{\gamma+1}\) is associated with the adjacent interval, whose \(\sup\) is \(\inf\) of associated interval of \(s_\gamma\).

It suffices to show the following claim, as it applies for \(\beta = 0, \alpha = \omega\):

Claim: For \(\beta < \alpha \leq \omega\), \(\sum_{\beta \leq \gamma < \alpha} \mu (s_{\gamma+1}, s_\gamma] = \mu (s_\alpha, s_\beta]\).

We show this by transfinite induction on \(\alpha\). It is obvious for \(\alpha = 0\).

Case\(\alpha = \alpha' + 1\): By induction hypothesis and definition of \(\mu\), one has \[LHS = \sum_{\beta \leq \gamma < \alpha'} \mu (s_{\gamma+1}, s_\gamma] + \mu (s_{\alpha'+1}, s_{\alpha'}] = \mu (s_{\alpha'}, s_\beta] + \mu (s_{\alpha'+1}, s_{\alpha'}] = \mu (s_{\alpha'+1}, s_\beta].\]

Case\(\alpha\) is a limit ordinal: Countable limit ordinals have cofinality \(\omega_0\), i.e., there is a sequence \((\alpha_j)\) increasing to \(\alpha\). We may add \(\alpha_0 = 0\) without losing generality. Then, \[LHS = \sum_{j=0}^\infty \sum_{\alpha_j \leq \gamma < \alpha_{j+1}} \mu (s_{\gamma+1}, s_\gamma] = \sum_{j=0}^\infty \mu (s_{\alpha_{j+1}}, s_{\alpha_j}] = \mu (s_\alpha, s_\beta]\] where the last summation follows by right-continuity of \(F\).

 ◻

By Theorem \(\ref{premeasure-induced-measure}\), premeasure defined above induces a unique measure associated with \(F\).

Definition 15. The unique induced measure is called the Lebesgue-Stieltjes measure associated with \(F\), often denoted \(\mu_F\).

Proposition 8. The above measure \(\mu\) satisfies

  1. (Outer regularity) \(\mu(E) = \inf \{ \mu(U) : U \supset E \text{ open} \}\).

  2. (Inner regularity) \(\mu(E) = \sup \{ \mu(K) : K \subset E \text{ compact} \}\).

Proof. Notice any open set is a countable union of open intervals. ◻

Integration

Nonnegative Integration

Fix a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M})\), and a measure \(\mu : \mathcal{F} \to [0, \infty]\). We may consider \[\begin{matrix} \tilde{\mu} : & [0, \infty] \otimes_\mathbb{N}\mathcal{F} & \to & [0, \infty] \\ & r \otimes f & \mapsto & r \cdot \mu(f) \end{matrix}\] which we may denote \(\int d\mu\) instead, or \(\int f := \tilde{\mu}(f)\) if the measure is clear.

Remark 8. \(\tilde{\mu}\) is linear, i.e., \(\int (f + g) = \int f + \int g\) and \(\int cf = c \int f\). It also retains the continuity from below.

Remark 9. There is the natural injection \([0, \infty] \otimes_\mathbb{N}\mathcal{F} \hookrightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Hom}}(X, [0, \infty])\). A function in its image is called a simple function, which is a linear combination of index functions.

Theorem 9. \(\tilde{\mu}\) extends naturally to countably additive \[\int d \mu : \mathop{\mathrm{Hom}}(X, [0, \infty]) \to [0, \infty].\]

Proof.

Step 1: Uniqueness of possible extension

It suffices to show \(f \in \mathop{\mathrm{Hom}}(X, [0, \infty])\) is a countable sum of \(\mathbb{R}\)-multiple of indicator functions, since countable additivity determines \(\int f d\mu\).

We may assume \(f < \infty\), since \(f = \infty \cdot 1_{f = \infty} + f \cdot 1_{f \neq \infty}\). For \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\), consider the \(n\)-th digit \[digit_n(y) = \left[ \frac{y}{2^n} \right] - 2 \left[ \frac{y}{2^{n+1}} \right] \in \{0, 1\}.\] Then, \(y = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} digit_n(y) \cdot 2^n\), and \(digit_n\) is clearly measurable. Hence, \(f = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} 2^n \cdot 1_{d_n \circ f = 1}\).

Step 2: Well-definedness as countable sum of simple functions

Considering partial sums, it is enough to show that for simple functions \(\varphi_i \uparrow f\), \(\lim_{i \to \infty} \int \varphi_i\) is independent of choice of \((\varphi_i)\).

Claim: \(\lim_i \int \varphi_i = \sup_{\psi \leq f} \int \psi\).

Fix \(\psi \leq f\) and \(\alpha \in (0, 1)\). Let \(E_i := \{ \varphi_i \geq \alpha f \}\). Note that \(1_{E_i} \uparrow 1_X\): for each \(x \in X\), \(\varphi_i(x) \geq \alpha f(x)\) for some \(i\). As \(\int \varphi_i \geq \alpha \int 1_{E_i} \psi\), taking limit, \(\lim_i \int \varphi_i \geq \alpha \int \psi\) by continuity from below. Taking supremum of RHS gives the result.

Step 3: Countable additivity in general

Countable additivity can be shown by decomposing into simple functions, and invoking previous step. That is, for \(f = \sum_{i \in I} f_i\), choose \(f_i = \sum_{j \in J_i} \varphi_{ij}\). Since the sum \(f = \sum_{i \in I, j \in J_j} \varphi_{ij}\) is countable, one has \[\sum_{i \in I} \int f_i = \sum_{i \in I, j \in J_j} \int \varphi_{ij} = \int f,\] as desired.

 ◻

Countable additivity gives the following as a direct corollary.

Corollary 10 (Monotone Convergence Theorem). If \(f_n \uparrow f\) for measurable functions, one has \(\int f_n \to \int f\) as \(n \to \infty\).
