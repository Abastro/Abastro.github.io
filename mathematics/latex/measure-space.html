Measure spaces

Posted on July 7, 2024

\(\sigma\)-algebra

Definition 1. \(\mathcal{A} \in \mathcal{P}(X)\) is

  • an algebra on \(X\) if it is closed under finite union and complement.

  • an \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(X\) if it is closed under countable union and complement.

Remark 1. An algebra is a \(\sigma\)-algebra if it is closed under disjoint countable union.

Consider a category of spaces given by

  1. Objects: \((X, \mathcal{E})\) for \(\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\).

  2. Morphisms: \((X, \mathcal{E}) \xrightarrow{f} (Y, \mathcal{F})\) s.t. \(A \in \mathcal{F}\) implies \(f^{-1} A \in \mathcal{E}\).

Definition 2. \((X, \mathcal{M})\) is called a measure space if \(\mathcal{M}\) is \(\sigma\)-algebra, and morphisms between such spaces are called measurable functions. Sets in \(\mathcal{M}\) is said to be measurable.

Definition 3. \(\mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{E})\) is the terminal \(\sigma\)-alg with a map \((X, \mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{E})) \to (X, \mathcal{E})\). In other words, it is the smallest \(\sigma\)-alg containing \(\mathcal{E}\).

Definition 4. A Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is \(\mathcal{B}_X := \mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{O})\) for collections of open sets \(\mathcal{O}\).

If not mentioned otherwise, a topological space will be given a measure space structure by taking Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra.

Proposition 1. Category of measure spaces have arbitrary product, i.e., \(\prod_{\alpha \in J} X_\alpha\) has a natural \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\bigotimes_{\alpha \in J} \mathcal{M}_\alpha\) with measurable projections \(\pi_\alpha : \prod X_\bullet \to X_\alpha\) for \((X_\alpha, \mathcal{M}_\alpha)_{\alpha \in J}\).

Proof. Take \(\bigotimes_{\alpha \in J} \mathcal{M}_\alpha := M(\bigcup_{\alpha \in J} \{\pi_{\alpha}^{-1} A \mid A \in \mathcal{M}_{\alpha}\})\), which is the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra with projections \(\pi_\alpha\). Then, the cone \(\{ (Y, \mathcal{N}) \to (X_\alpha, \mathcal{M}_\alpha) \}_\alpha\) factors through this space. ◻

Remark 2. If index set is countable, then \(\bigotimes \mathcal{M}_\bullet = \{ \prod E_\bullet | E_\alpha \in \mathcal{E}_\alpha \}\).

Remark 3. For metric spaces given Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra, the projections \(\prod_I X_\bullet \to X_i\) are measurable for finite \(I\), but \(\prod_I X_\bullet\) is only the product if each \(X_i\) has a countable dense subset.

Counts and Measures

Counts

Definition 5. The set of count functions, \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), is the set of bounded measurable functions \(X \to \mathbb{N}\), where \(X\) is given an algebra \(\mathcal{A}\).

Example 1. An indicator function \(1_A\) of a measurable set \(A\) is a count function \[1_A(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & x \in A \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

Proposition 2. \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\) is closed under (finite) addition. Further, if \(\mathcal{A}\) is a \(\sigma\)-alg, it is closed under countable addition whenever bounded.

Proof. Consider \[\left\{ \sum_{i \in I} f_i \leq M \right\} = \bigcup_{M = \sum_{i \in I} m_i} \bigcap_{i \in I} \{ f_i \leq m_i \}.\] If \(I\) is finite, the union and intersection are finite, while if \(I\) is countable, both are countable. It follows that the finite sum is measurable for algebra, and countable sum is measurable for \(\sigma\)-algebra. ◻

Remark 4. Indicator functions serve as a \(\mathbb{N}\)-basis of \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}(X)\).

We give a partial order on \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}(X)\), where \(f \leq g\) if \(f(x) \leq g(x)\) on all \(x \in X\). Then, \(g-f \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}(X)\) if and only if \(f \leq g\).

Proposition 3. Suppose \(\mathcal{F}\) is a set of bounded functions \(f: X \to \mathbb{N}\), closed under addition, and \(f - g \in \mathcal{F}\) for \(f \geq g \in \mathcal{F}\). If \(f \in \mathcal{F}\) implies \(1_{f = 0} \in \mathcal{F}\), such \(\mathcal{F}\) uniquely determines algebra \(\mathcal{A}\) such that \(\mathcal{F} = \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\). Further, if \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under bounded countable addition, then \(\mathcal{A}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.

Proof. Consider \(\mathcal{A} := \{ \{f > 0\} \mid f \in \mathcal{F} \}\), which is an algebra:

  • (Closed under union) \(\{f>0\} \cup \{g>0\} = \{f+g>0\}\),

  • (Closed under complement) \(\{ 1_{f=0} > 0 \} = \{f > 0\}^c\).

Note that if \(E \in \mathcal{A}\), then \(E = \{f > 0\}^c = \{f = 0\}\) for some \(f \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(1_E \in \mathcal{F}\).

It suffices to show that \(\mathcal{F} = \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), since \(\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}\) determines \(\mathcal{A}\) through indicator functions.

  1. Suppose \(g \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)\), then \(\{g \geq i\} \in \mathcal{A}\) gives \(1_{g \geq i} \in \mathcal{F}\). Therefore, \(g = \sum_{i=1}^\infty 1_{g \geq i} \in \mathcal{F}\).

  2. Suppose \(g \in \mathcal{F}\). \(g \geq 1_{g > 0} \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(g - 1_{g>0} \in \mathcal{F}\). By induction on bounds, \(\{ g \geq i+1 \} = \{ (g - 1_{g>0}) \geq i \} \in \mathcal{A}\) for each \(i \in \mathbb{N}\). As \(\{g \geq 0\} = X\), this shows that \(\{g \geq j\}\) is measurable, so \(g\) is \(\mathcal{A}\)-measurable.

Lastly, suppose \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under bounded countable addition as well. Then for disjoint sets \(A_i \in \mathcal{A}\), \(1_{\bigsqcup_i^\infty A_i} = \sum_i^\infty 1_{A_i} \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(\bigsqcup_i^\infty A_i \in \mathcal{A}\). Since \(\mathcal{A}\) is established as an algebra, this shows that it is a \(\sigma\)-algebra. ◻

Measures

Definition 6. Given a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M})\), a measure is a map \[\mu : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{M}}(X) \to [0, \infty]\] which is countably additive, i.e., \(\mu(\sum_I f_\bullet) = \sum_I \mu(f_\bullet)\) for at most countable \(I\) whenever defined. Traditional measure can be recovered as \(\mu(A) := \mu(1_A)\).

Note that \(\mu(f) = \sum_k k \cdot \mu(f^{-1}(k))\), so it is easy to see the equivalence of the definitions.

A measure \(\mu\) is trivially monotone, i.e., \(\mu(f) \leq \mu(g)\) if \(f \leq g\).

Proposition 4. A measure \(\mu\) also has properties

  1. (Continuity from above) If \(f_j \uparrow f_\infty\), \(\lim_{j \to \infty} \mu(f_j) = \mu(f_\infty)\).

  2. (Continuity from below) If \(f_j \downarrow f_\infty\) and \(\mu(f_1) < \infty\), \(\lim_{j \to \infty} \mu(f_j) = \mu(f_\infty)\).

Proof. First one follows from countable addition of differences. The second is simply a modification of the first using \(g_j = f_1 - f_j\), where \(\mu(f_1) < \infty\) is required for the limit. ◻

Couple of basic terms to describe measures and its sets.

Definition 7 (Finiteness of a measure). A measure \(\mu\) is

  • finite if \(\mu < \infty\).

  • \(\sigma\)-finite if a countable cover \((E_i)\) has \(\mu|_{E_i} < \infty\).

  • semi-finite if every msb set has a subset of measure \((0, \infty)\).

Definition 8. \(E\) is (\(\mu\)-)null set if \(\mu(E) = 0\).

Definition 9. \(P(x)\) holds (\(\mu\)-)almost everywhere if \(\mu\{ x \mid \neg P(x) \} = 0\).

Remark 5. This gives an alternative notion of equality on \(\hom(X, Y)\): That is, \(f\) and \(g\) is "roughly equal" if \(f = g\) almost everywhere.

Constructions of a measure

Usually a measure is constructed as an extension. Given a map \(\mu : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X) \to [0, \infty]\) with properties, an extension of it on \(\mathcal{M}\) is a map (measure) \(\bar{\mu} : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{M}}(X) \to [0, \infty]\) such that \(\bar{\mu}|_{\mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X)} = \mu\).

Completion

Definition 10. \(\mu\) is complete if any subset of \(\mu\)-null set is measurable.

Proposition 5. Consider a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)\). Then, \[\bar{\mathcal{M}} := \left\{ F \in \mathcal{P}(X) \mid E \subset F \subset E' \text{ for } E, E' \in \mathcal{M}, \mu(E' \setminus E) = 0 \right\}\] is a \(\sigma\)-algebra, and \(\mu\) has a unique extension \(\bar{\mu}\) on \(\bar{\mathcal{M}}\) such that \(\bar{\mu}\) is complete.

Proof. Both countable and finite union preserves the condition of \(\bar{\mathcal{M}}\), since countable union of \(\mu\)-null set is \(\mu\)-null. Complement also preserves \(\mathcal{M}\), since \(E^c \setminus E'^c = E' \setminus E\). Hence, \(\bar{\mathcal{M}}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.

Now, being a complete extension mandates \(\bar{\mu}(F) = \mu(E)\) for \(E \subset F \subset E'\) in the condition of \(\mathcal{M}\). This gives well-defined measure on \(\mathcal{M}\), since \(\mu(E)\) is determined by \(F\). ◻

Definition 11. The measure \(\bar{\mu}\) is called the completion of \(\mu\).

Premeasure extension

Definition 12. For an algebra \(\mathcal{A}\), a premeasure on it is \[\mu_0 : \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}(X) \to \mathbb{R}\] that satisfies precountable additivity: if \(\sum_I f_\bullet \in \mathop{\mathrm{Cnt}}_{\mathcal{A}}\), \[\mu_0 \left( \sum_I f_\bullet \right) = \sum_I \mu_0(f_\bullet).\]

Definition 13. \(\mu^* : \operatorname{Mor}(X, \mathbb{N}) \to [0, \infty]\) is an outer measure if it is monotone and countably subadditive.

Theorem 6. For \(\mathcal{M} = \mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{A})\) and a \(\sigma\)-finite premeasure \(\mu_0\) on \(\mathcal{A}\), one has the unique extension measure \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{M}\).

Nonnegative Integration

Fix a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M})\), and a measure \(\mu : \mathcal{F} \to [0, \infty]\). We may consider \[\begin{array}{cccc} \tilde{\mu}: & [0, \infty] \otimes_\mathbb{N}\mathcal{F} & \rightarrow & [0, \infty] \\ & r \otimes f & \mapsto & r \cdot \mu(f) \\ \end{array}\] which we may denote \(\int d\mu\) instead, or \(\int f := \tilde{\mu}(f)\) if the measure is obvious.

Remark 6. \(\tilde{\mu}\) is linear, i.e., \(\int (f + g) = \int f + \int g\) and \(\int cf = c \int f\). It also retains the continuity from below.

Remark 7. There is the natural injection \([0, \infty] \otimes_\mathbb{N}\mathcal{F} \hookrightarrow \hom(X, [0, \infty])\). A function in its image is called a simple function, which is a linear combination of index functions.

Theorem 7. \(\tilde{\mu}\) extends naturally to countably additive \[\int d \mu : \hom(X, [0, \infty]) \to [0, \infty].\]

Proof.

Step 1: Uniqueness of possible extension

It suffices to show \(f \in \hom(X, [0, \infty])\) is a countable sum of \(\mathbb{R}\)-multiple of indicator functions, since countable additivity determines \(\int f d\mu\).

We may assume \(f < \infty\), since \(f = \infty \cdot 1_{f = \infty} + f \cdot 1_{f \neq \infty}\). For \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\), consider the \(n\)-th digit \[digit_n(y) = \left[ \frac{y}{2^n} \right] - 2 \left[ \frac{y}{2^{n+1}} \right] \in \{0, 1\}.\] Then, \(y = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} digit_n(y) \cdot 2^n\), and \(digit_n\) is clearly measurable. Hence, \(f = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} 2^n \cdot 1_{d_n \circ f = 1}\).

Step 2: Well-definedness as countable sum of simple functions

Considering partial sums, it is enough to show that for simple functions \(\varphi_i \uparrow f\), \(\lim_{i \to \infty} \int \varphi_i\) is independent of choice of \((\varphi_i)\).

Fix \(\psi \leq f\) and \(\alpha \in (0, 1)\). Let \(E_i := \{ \varphi_i \geq \alpha f \}\). Note that \(1_{E_i} \uparrow 1_X\): for each \(x \in X\), \(\varphi_i(x) \geq \alpha f(x)\) for some \(i\). As \(\int \varphi_i \geq \alpha \int 1_{E_i} \psi\), taking limit, \(\lim_i \int \varphi_i \geq \alpha \int \psi\) by continuity from below. Taking supremum of RHS gives the result.

Step 3: Countable additivity in general

Countable additivity can be shown by decomposing into simple functions, and invoking previous step. That is, for \(f = \sum_{i \in I} f_i\), choose \(f_i = \sum_{j \in J_i} \varphi_{ij}\). Since the sum \(f = \sum_{i \in I, j \in J_j} \varphi_{ij}\) is countable, one has \[\sum_{i \in I} \int f_i = \sum_{i \in I, j \in J_j} \int \varphi_{ij} = \int f,\] as desired.

 ◻

Countable additivity gives the following as a direct corollary.

Corollary 8 (Monotone Convergence Theorem). If \(f_n \uparrow f\) for measurable functions, one has \(\int f_n \to \int f\) as \(n \to \infty\).
